Web Design Principles

Psychology-driven principles for premium, high-converting websites.

Users form a lasting opinion within 50 milliseconds. A polished site makes visitors assume the product is equally high quality.

Apply it

  • Lead with a strong hero — clean layout, clear value prop, quality imagery
  • Typography and spacing should feel intentional from the first viewport
  • Remove clutter that doesn't support the first impression
  • Speed matters — over 3 seconds and the first impression is "slow"

Cognitive fluency is how easily the brain processes what it sees. High fluency = more trust. Every extra element adds cognitive load. Premium sites feel effortless.

Apply it

  • 2 typefaces maximum (1 heading, 1 body)
  • Restricted palette — 1 primary, 1 accent, 2–3 neutrals
  • Break info into scannable chunks with generous whitespace
  • Use visual hierarchy so users never "figure out" where to look

People judge experiences by their peak moment and the end, not the average. Micro interactions create those peaks — a smooth hover, a satisfying button animation, a slick transition.

Apply it

  • Subtle hover effects on buttons, cards, links
  • Smooth scroll behaviour and page transitions
  • Animate elements into view on scroll (restrained)
  • Make the CTA feel special — spring physics on hover/press
  • End well — clean footer, thank-you animation on submit

Apple — Premium Simplicity

One product per viewport, massive whitespace, minimal copy. Slow-reveal scroll animations and cinema-quality photography.

Luxury Brands & White Space

Extreme whitespace communicates exclusivity — "we don't need noise to get your attention." Reduces cognitive load to near zero.

Stripe & Figma — Clarity for Complex Products

Progressive disclosure, clean typography hierarchies, and micro interactions that make abstract concepts tangible.

Step 1 — Engineer the First Impression

Design hero first. Single headline, one supporting line, one CTA. Remove everything else.

Step 2 — Reduce Cognitive Load

Audit every element: does it serve the user's primary goal? Simplify nav to 5–7 items. Consistent spacing. Let whitespace work.

Step 3 — Add Micro Interactions

Once structure is clean, layer in delight. Hover states, scroll reveals, loading transitions. These separate amateur from professional.

Users don't remember features — they remember how a site made them feel.

Social Media Marketing

Instagram strategy, posting cadence, templates, and growth tactics.

  • Promotional — templates, launches, offers. KPIs: link clicks, conversions
  • Free-Value — guides, checklists. KPIs: downloads, email sign-ups
  • Case Studies — before/after. KPIs: engagement, inquiries
  • Testimonials — client quotes, UGC. KPIs: trust, profile visits
  • Behind-the-Scenes — process, team. KPIs: comments, follower growth
  • Tips & Tutorials — UX tips, how-tos. KPIs: saves, shares
  • Reels — 15–30s videos. Algorithm favours these. KPIs: views, reach

2–3 feed posts/week + daily Stories.

  • Monday — Promotional or case study
  • Thursday — Tips/tutorial carousel or Reel
  • Weekend — Behind-the-scenes or testimonial
  • Daily — Stories (polls, Q&As, process clips)

"Pro Tip: Light-coloured CTAs get 20% more clicks. Try it!"

Organic ($0)

Quality visuals, hashtags, Stories stickers, reply to everything. Cross-promote. +5–15% followers/month.

$200/month

1–2 boosted posts, lookalike audiences. 100–500 clicks, 100–300 new followers.

$1000/month

Feed + Reels ads, A/B testing, 1–2 mid-tier influencers. 500–1000+ new followers, dozens of leads.

  • Follower Growth — new per week, track after campaigns
  • Engagement Rate — (likes+comments+saves+shares)/impressions. Average 1–3%
  • Profile CTR — link taps. High = strong CTAs
  • Leads — emails, DMs, form submissions

A/B test ideas

  • Image vs carousel vs Reel on same topic
  • Short vs long captions
  • "Link in bio" vs "DM us"
  • Broad vs niche hashtags
  • Morning vs evening posting
